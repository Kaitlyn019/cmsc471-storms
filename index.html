Storms over Time

Storms, as an atmospheric events, have the formidable power of affecting the surface and strongly implying severe weather. They have the potential to harm lives and property in storm surges, heavy rain or snow causing flooding, and plenty of other natural hazards. Hence, learning more about this dangerous enemy to find its pattern over time could be a guarder for human activities and better living protection in experientialism. For example, is it getting a higher probability of happening in our world?

Unfortunately, the answer is positive. From 1975 to 2020, we see there is an apparent observation of increment of storms number over time.

Therefore, focusing on the changes in more detailed features could be more effective for us to know this beast.















Where the Storms are Hitting

We see that there are more storms every year, but where exactly are they hitting?

The map here shows the locations of every storm from 1975 to 2020. The color represents the year of the storm, and the size represents the category. Storms that are of category -1 or category 0 do not count as hurricanes. We can see where these storms are reaching. They get sparser as you go farther north or farther east. However, if we break it down by year, this actually changes.































Let's consider...

Storms from 1975 to 1990

Here are all the storms from before 1990, as well as the blue path which represents the average path traveled by these storms. This path was calculated by averaging the longtitude and lattitudes of the starting and ending points of the storms, and the storms in-between. Unsurprisingly, most of these storms are centered in the middle of the ocean.

Storms are fairly sparse when you go farther out though, unlike the density we saw in our previous map. Let's go farther in time.
